# Class Pulse
## A Story About Not Leaving Kids Behind in the Moment They Need Us Most

---

# The Moment That Started Everything

Imagine a 5th grader — let's call her Maya.

She nods along during the math lesson.
She doesn't raise her hand.
She doesn't want to look lost in front of her friends.

And by the time her teacher finds out she didn't understand? 
The lesson is over. The week has moved on. The gap has grown.

**This happened every single day. We decided to do something about it.**

---

# Section 1: Opening & Framing
## *The Reality We Were Living In*

---

# What We Were Seeing

In our 5th-grade FL math classroom, students were struggling in silence.

Not because they didn't try.
Not because their teacher didn't care.

But because the system we worked inside had no way of surfacing confusion **in the moment it was happening.**

The only data we had was yesterday's quiz. And yesterday is too late.

---

# The Practice We Set Out to Change

We were working in a model where **assessment happened after learning** — not during it.

And for students already behind grade level, that delay didn't just slow progress. It compounded it. Confusion became frustration. Frustration became disengagement. Disengagement became behavior.

**We asked: What if we could close that gap — in real time, with no one feeling singled out?**

---

# What We Believed Was Possible

We believed that if a teacher could know, *in the middle of the lesson*, which students were lost — they could do something about it **right then.**

Not tomorrow.
Not next week.
Right now.

And we believed that student — Maya — deserved to get support before she decided she "just wasn't a math person."

**The question wasn't whether this mattered. It was whether we were willing to build it.**

---

# Who This Was Always About

- **Students** who were silently slipping through the cracks, one lesson at a time.
- **Teachers** who were working incredibly hard and still feeling like they weren't reaching everyone.
- **Families** who trusted us with their children's futures every single morning.

The system wasn't failing these people because no one cared.
It was failing because no one had the right tool to act fast enough.

**We had influence over the tool. So we used it.**

---

# Section 2: The Work
## *What We Did and Why It Mattered*

---

# Our Vision — In Plain Language

We wanted to build something that would let a teacher know — instantly, quietly, without any student being embarrassed — when a child was lost.

And we wanted the tool to meet the teacher where they were:
Moving around the room. Phone in hand. Mid-sentence.

**The vision was simple:** 
Help teachers reach every student in the moment it matters.
Not after. Not eventually.
**Now.**

---

# How We Did It — The Human Version

We sat with teachers.
We listened to what exhausted them.
We watched what happened when a student's hand didn't go up even when they were drowning.

We built small.
We tested in real classrooms, with real kids, during real lessons.
We adjusted when it didn't feel right.
We kept the student always at the center of every decision.

**The work was not a product launch. It was an act of care.**

---

# The Relationships That Made This Real

None of this would have been possible without:

- Teachers who trusted us enough to open their classrooms.
- Students who were willing to signal their confusion — even just by tapping a screen.
- School leaders who believed that innovation and care are not opposites.
- The **TFA SSP community** that gave us a framework to hold ourselves accountable.

**This was never a solo effort. It never is.**

---

# How We Knew If It Was Working

We monitored progress through two lenses:

**Quantitative:** Were more students getting support in real time? Were teachers completing more Checks for Understanding each day?

**Qualitative:** Were teachers feeling more present? Were students less anxious about showing they didn't understand?

We checked in weekly. We adjusted constantly. We never stopped asking: *Is this actually helping Maya?*

---

# Section 3: What We Found
## *The Climax — Where We Landed*

---

# What the Data Showed

When teachers had access to real-time comprehension signals, things changed:

- **Students received intervention in under 60 seconds** of signaling confusion — not the next day.
- Teachers reported completing **30% more daily Checks for Understanding.**
- Visible off-task behavior during guided practice dropped dramatically.

But the number that moved us most?

**The number of times a student raised a green signal — "I Got It" — after receiving a targeted 30-second intervention they didn't have to ask for.**

---

# What Teachers Said
*(feedback shared with permission, names removed)*

> *"I used to find out who was lost at the end of the unit. Now I find out in real time — and I can do something about it."*

> *"The AI gave me a 30-second script to explain fractions using pizza. The student's signal went green. That moment felt like everything."*

> *"For the first time, I feel like I didn't leave anyone behind today."*

---

# What We Noticed in Students

Students stopped performing understanding.
They started trusting that it was safe to say "I'm still working on it."

That trust — **the willingness of a 10-year-old to be honest about where they are** —
is one of the most powerful things a classroom can hold.

We did not manufacture it.
We built the conditions for it.

**And that made all the difference.**

---

# Synthesis: Looking Back Honestly

**What we're proud of:**
We built something that worked. In real classrooms. With real kids. That's rare.

**What we'd do differently:**
We'd involve students even earlier in the design process. Their instincts about what felt safe and engaging were right every time.

**What kept us from doing more:**
Time. Systems change takes longer than a school year — and we always knew that going in.

**What gave us strength anyway:**
The teachers. The students. The belief that we were doing exactly the right thing.

---

# Will This Last?

This initiative endures because it is not built on our presence.
It is built on a practice — a habit of asking *who is lost right now, and what do I do about it.*

That question doesn't require our tool to exist.
But we built the tool to make answering it easier.

The teachers who used it now ask that question differently.
The students who used it now expect to be reached in time.

**That shift — in expectation, in culture, in trust — that is the enduring change.**

---

# The Evidence We Carry

We carry it in data.
In timestamps and signals and records of students who received support in real time.

But we also carry it in something harder to measure:

A teacher who said, *"I didn't leave anyone behind today."*

A student who signaled green for the first time all semester.

A classroom where confusion became a signal — not a secret.

**That is Class Pulse. That is the work. That is why it matters.**
